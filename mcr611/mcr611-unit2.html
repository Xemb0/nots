1. Establishment, Spreading, and Tissue Damage

1.1 Establishment of Infection

Establishment means bacteria successfully enter and start living in the host body. This is the first step of infection. Bacteria must overcome the body's natural defenses to establish themselves.

Key Steps in Establishment:
  • Entry: Bacteria enter through skin, mouth, nose, or wounds
  • Attachment: Bacteria stick to host cells using adhesins
  • Multiplication: Bacteria start dividing and growing
  • Resistance: Bacteria fight against host defenses

1.2 Spreading of Infection

Spreading means bacteria move from the initial infection site to other parts of the body. They can spread through blood, lymph, or by direct extension to nearby tissues.

1.3 Tissue Damage

Tissue damage happens when bacteria destroy or harm host cells and tissues. This can occur through toxins, enzymes, or direct cell invasion.

2. Antiphagocytic Factors

Antiphagocytic factors are substances or structures that help bacteria avoid being eaten by phagocytes (immune cells that eat bacteria). These factors protect bacteria from the immune system.

Types of Antiphagocytic Factors:
  • Capsule: Gel-like layer around bacteria (e.g., Streptococcus pneumoniae)
  • M Protein: Surface protein that prevents phagocytosis (e.g., Group A Streptococcus)
  • Protein A: Binds to antibodies incorrectly, confusing immune system (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus)
  • Leukocidins: Toxins that kill white blood cells
  • Biofilm: Community of bacteria protected by slime layer

3. Mechanism of Bacterial Adhesion, Colonization, and Invasion

3.1 Bacterial Adhesion

Adhesion means bacteria stick to host cells. This is the first step before infection. Bacteria use special molecules called adhesins to attach to receptors on host cells.

4. Invasion of Mucous Membranes: Respiratory, Enteric, and Urogenital Tracts

4.1 Respiratory Tract

The respiratory tract includes nose, throat, and lungs. Bacteria enter through breathing. They attach to cells in the nose, throat, or lungs and cause infections like pneumonia, bronchitis, or sinusitis.

Depolymerizing enzymes break down large molecules in host tissues. They help bacteria spread by dissolving barriers. These enzymes break down proteins, carbohydrates, and other tissue components.

5.4 Variation

Variation means bacteria can change their characteristics. This helps them survive and avoid the immune system. Bacteria can change their surface proteins, making it hard for the immune system to recognize them.

  • Antigenic Variation: Change surface antigens to avoid immune recognition

5.5 Virulence

Virulence means how strong and dangerous a bacteria is. High virulence means bacteria cause severe disease easily. Low virulence means bacteria cause mild or no disease. Virulence depends on many factors like toxins, enzymes, and ability to spread.

6.4 Immune Response Overview

The immune response is how the body fights infections. It has two parts: innate (immediate, non-specific) and adaptive (slower, specific, with memory). Both work together to protect the body.
