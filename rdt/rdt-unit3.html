1.1 Plasmids (рдкреНрд▓рд╛рдЬреНрдорд┐рдб)

Plasmids are small, circular, double-stranded DNA molecules that are found naturally in bacteria. They are separate from the main bacterial chromosome and can replicate independently. Plasmids are like "mini chromosomes" that bacteria can share with each other.

1.4 Shuttle Vectors

Shuttle vectors are designed to replicate in two or more different host organisms. They contain multiple origins of replication, allowing them to "shuttle" between different cell types.

Examples:
  • E. coli - Yeast shuttle: Contains bacterial ori and yeast 2╬╝ ori
  • E. coli - Mammalian shuttle: Contains bacterial ori and SV40 ori

Applications: Useful for expressing eukaryotic genes in bacteria first, then transferring to eukaryotic cells for functional studies.

1.5 Expression Vectors

Expression vectors are designed to enable the expression (transcription and translation) of the inserted gene in the host cell.

Essential Components:
  • Promoter: Strong, regulated promoter (lac, trp, T7, etc.)
  • Ribosome Binding Site (RBS): For efficient translation initiation
  • Terminator: Transcription termination signal
  • Tag sequences: For protein purification (His-tag, GST-tag)

Types:

  • Bacterial expression vectors: pET series, pBAD series
  • Yeast expression vectors: pYES2, pPIC series
  • Mammalian expression vectors: pcDNA3, pCMV series

1.6 Promoter Probe Vectors

Promoter probe vectors are specialized vectors used to identify and characterize promoter sequences. They contain:

  • A reporter gene (lacZ, GFP, luciferase) without a promoter
  • Multiple cloning site upstream of the reporter gene
  • When a promoter sequence is inserted, it drives reporter gene expression

Applications: Studying gene regulation, identifying promoter regions, analyzing promoter strength

2. Cloning Strategies: Introduction into Bacterial Cells

Once a recombinant DNA molecule is constructed, it must be introduced into a host cell for replication and expression. Several methods are used to introduce DNA into bacterial cells.

2.1 Transformation

Transformation is the process by which bacteria take up foreign DNA from their environment. It was the first method developed for introducing recombinant DNA into bacteria.

Types of Transformation:
  • Natural Transformation: Some bacteria (e.g., Bacillus, Streptococcus) naturally take up DNA
  • Artificial Transformation: E. coli requires treatment to become competent

2.1.2 Transformation Procedure

  1. Mix competent cells with plasmid DNA (or ligation mixture)
  2. Incubate on ice for 30 minutes
  3. Heat shock at 42┬░C for 30-90 seconds
  4. Return to ice for 2 minutes
  5. Add recovery medium (LB broth) and incubate at 37┬░C for 1 hour
  6. Plate on selective agar medium containing antibiotic
  7. Incubate overnight at 37┬░C
  8. Transformants will grow as colonies

2.2 Other Methods for DNA Introduction

2.2.1 Transduction

Transduction involves using bacteriophages to deliver DNA into bacterial cells. The DNA is packaged into phage particles and introduced during infection.

2.2.2 Conjugation

Conjugation is the direct transfer of DNA from one bacterium to another through cell-to-cell contact via a sex pilus. It requires special conjugation vectors with tra genes.

2.2.3 Electroporation

As mentioned above, electroporation uses high-voltage electric pulses to create temporary pores in cell membranes, allowing DNA entry.

3. Selection of Clones

After transformation, only a small fraction of cells will contain the recombinant DNA of interest. Selection methods are used to identify and isolate these clones.

3.1 Antibiotic Selection

The most common initial selection method. Cells containing plasmids with antibiotic resistance genes will survive on antibiotic-containing media, while non-transformed cells die.

Example:

If plasmid contains ampicillin resistance gene (ampR):

  • Plate transformed cells on ampicillin-containing agar
  • Only cells with plasmid will grow
  • Non-transformed cells cannot grow

3.3 Colony Hybridization

Colony hybridization (also called colony lift or Grunstein-Hogness method) is used to identify colonies containing a specific DNA sequence.

Procedure:
  1. Grow transformed colonies on agar plate
  2. Place nitrocellulose or nylon membrane on colonies to transfer
  3. Remove membrane (colonies stick to it)
  4. Lyse cells and denature DNA on membrane
  5. Fix DNA to membrane (baking or UV crosslinking)
  6. Hybridize with labeled probe (radioactive or fluorescent)
  7. Wash to remove unbound probe
  8. Detect signal (autoradiography or fluorescence)
  9. Match signal position to original colony position
  10. Pick positive colony and grow for further analysis

3.4 Detection of Translation Products

When the inserted gene is expressed as a protein, various methods can detect the translation products.

Methods:
  • SDS-PAGE: Separate proteins by size, detect by staining
  • Western Blotting: Detect specific proteins using antibodies
  • Enzyme Assays: If protein has enzymatic activity
  • Functional Assays: Test for biological activity

3.5 Immunological Methods

Immunological detection methods use antibodies to identify specific proteins.

3.5.1 Western Blotting

  1. Separate proteins by SDS-PAGE
  2. Transfer proteins to membrane (nitrocellulose/PVDF)
  3. Block non-specific binding sites
  4. Incubate with primary antibody (specific to target protein)
  5. Wash and incubate with secondary antibody (labeled)
  6. Detect signal (chemiluminescence, fluorescence, colorimetric)

3.5.2 Immunoprecipitation

Antibodies bound to target protein are captured using protein A/G beads, allowing purification and detection.

3.5.3 ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay)

Antibodies are used to detect and quantify specific proteins in cell lysates or culture supernatants.

4.1 Types of Probes

4.1.1 DNA Probes

Double-stranded or single-stranded DNA molecules that are complementary to the target sequence.

  • Genomic DNA probes: Derived from genomic DNA
  • cDNA probes: Derived from complementary DNA (reverse transcribed from mRNA)
  • PCR-generated probes: Produced by PCR amplification
  • Synthetic oligonucleotide probes: Short, chemically synthesized DNA (15-50 bp)

4.1.2 RNA Probes

RNA molecules (riboprobes) synthesized in vitro using RNA polymerases. Often more sensitive than DNA probes due to RNA-DNA hybrid stability.

4.1.3 Oligonucleotide Probes

Short, single-stranded DNA sequences (15-50 nucleotides) that are chemically synthesized. Used when the protein sequence is known but DNA sequence is unknown.

Advantages of Oligonucleotide Probes:
  • Can be synthesized quickly
  • Specific and sensitive
  • Can be designed from amino acid sequences
  • Uniform quality and purity

4.2.1 Nick Translation

Uses DNase I to create nicks and DNA polymerase I to replace nucleotides with labeled ones.

4.2.2 Random Priming

Random hexanucleotides prime DNA synthesis, incorporating labeled nucleotides during polymerization.

4.2.3 End Labeling

T4 polynucleotide kinase adds labeled phosphate to 5' ends, or terminal transferase adds nucleotides to 3' ends.

4.2.4 PCR Labeling

PCR amplification in the presence of labeled nucleotides incorporates labels during amplification.

4.3 Strategies for Construction of Probes

4.3.1 From Known DNA Sequence

  1. If target DNA sequence is known, synthesize oligonucleotide probe directly
  2. Or PCR amplify known sequence and label during PCR
  3. Or isolate restriction fragment and label by nick translation or random priming

4.3.2 From Protein Sequence

  1. Determine amino acid sequence of protein
  2. Use genetic code to design degenerate oligonucleotide probes
  3. Account for codon degeneracy (multiple codons for same amino acid)
  4. Synthesize probe mixture or use inosine at wobble positions

4.3.3 From Homologous Genes

Use probes from related species or genes. Cross-hybridization may occur with related sequences.

4.3.4 Subtractive Hybridization

Create probe by removing common sequences between two samples, enriching for unique sequences.

4.4 Use of ESTs as Probes

ESTs (Expressed Sequence Tags) are short sequences (200-800 bp) derived from cDNA clones. They represent portions of expressed genes.

Advantages of ESTs as Probes:
  • Represent expressed genes (not introns or intergenic regions)
  • Quick to obtain (single-pass sequencing of cDNA)
  • Large databases available (dbEST)
  • Can identify tissue-specific or condition-specific genes
  • Useful for gene discovery

4.4.1 EST Database Mining

  1. Search EST databases (dbEST, Unigene) for sequences of interest
  2. Identify ESTs from target tissue or condition
  3. Obtain EST clone or amplify EST sequence
  4. Label and use as probe for screening libraries or Northern blotting

4.5 Use of cDNA as Probes

cDNA (complementary DNA) is DNA synthesized from mRNA template using reverse transcriptase. cDNA probes are widely used because they:

Advantages:
  • Represent expressed genes only (no introns)
  • Can be full-length or partial
  • Directly relate to protein-coding sequences
  • Useful for Northern blotting, library screening, microarray studies
  • Can be tissue-specific or developmental stage-specific

4.5.1 Construction of cDNA Probes

  1. RNA Extraction: Isolate total RNA or mRNA from tissue/cells
  2. Reverse Transcription: Use reverse transcriptase to synthesize first strand cDNA
  3. Second Strand Synthesis: Convert RNA-DNA hybrid to double-stranded cDNA
  4. PCR Amplification (optional): If specific gene, use gene-specific primers
  5. Labeling: Label cDNA by nick translation, random priming, or PCR
  6. Purification: Remove unincorporated labels before use

4.5.2 Applications of cDNA Probes

  • Library Screening: Identify full-length genes from genomic or cDNA libraries
  • Northern Blotting: Detect mRNA expression levels and sizes
  • In Situ Hybridization: Localize gene expression in tissues
  • Microarray Analysis: Study gene expression patterns
  • RFLP Analysis: Detect polymorphisms
