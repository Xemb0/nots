1. Metabolic Pathways and Metabolic Control Mechanisms

What are Metabolic Pathways?

Metabolic pathways are sequences of chemical reactions that occur inside cells. These reactions are controlled by enzymes (special proteins that speed up reactions). Think of them as a series of steps that convert one molecule into another, like an assembly line in a factory.

Types of Metabolic Pathways:

a) Catabolic Pathways (Breaking Down)

These pathways break down complex molecules into simpler ones and release energy. Example: Breaking down glucose (sugar) to get energy.

b) Anabolic Pathways (Building Up)

These pathways build complex molecules from simpler ones and consume energy. Example: Making proteins from amino acids.

2. Primary and Secondary Metabolites

Primary Metabolites

Primary metabolites are essential compounds that cells need for growth, reproduction, and survival. They are produced during the growth phase and are necessary for basic cell functions.

Examples of Primary Metabolites:
  • Amino Acids: Building blocks of proteins (e.g., glycine, alanine)
  • Nucleotides: Building blocks of DNA and RNA (e.g., ATP, GTP)
  • Carbohydrates: Energy sources (e.g., glucose, starch)
  • Vitamins: Essential nutrients (e.g., B-complex vitamins)

Secondary Metabolites

Secondary metabolites are compounds that are NOT essential for basic cell functions but help organisms survive in their environment. They are often produced after the growth phase and serve ecological functions like defense, competition, or communication.

Examples of Secondary Metabolites:
  • Antibiotics: Fight against other microorganisms (e.g., penicillin, streptomycin)
  • Pigments: Provide color (e.g., carotenoids, melanin)
  • Toxins: Defense mechanisms (e.g., botulinum toxin)
  • Alkaloids: Chemical defense (e.g., caffeine, nicotine)

3. Biotechnological Innovations in the Chemical Industry

Biotechnology has revolutionized the chemical industry by using living organisms (microbes) and their enzymes to produce chemicals. This is more efficient, environmentally friendly, and cost-effective than traditional chemical methods.

What are Biocatalysts? Biocatalysts are enzymes or whole cells that speed up chemical reactions. They work like tiny machines that make reactions happen faster and more efficiently.

Understanding P/O Ratio:

What is ATP?

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) is like the "energy currency" of cells. Just like money is used to buy things, ATP is used to power cellular processes.

During oxidative phosphorylation (the process that makes ATP), electrons flow through a chain and create a proton gradient. This gradient is used to make ATP. The P/O ratio tells us how many ATP molecules are produced per oxygen atom reduced.
