1. Isolation of Chromosomal and Plasmid DNA from Bacteria and Yeast

1.1 Isolation of Chromosomal DNA from Bacteria

Isolating bacterial chromosomal DNA is the first step in genetic engineering. The process involves breaking open bacterial cells and separating DNA from other cellular components.

Steps for Bacterial Chromosomal DNA Isolation:
  1. Cell Harvesting: Centrifuge bacterial culture to collect cells
  2. Cell Lysis: Break open cells using:
    • Physical methods: Freeze-thaw, sonication, or mechanical disruption
    • Chemical methods: Lysozyme (breaks cell wall) + detergents (SDS) to break membrane
  3. Protein Removal: Add proteinase K and incubate to digest proteins
  4. DNA Extraction: Use phenol-chloroform extraction to separate DNA from proteins
  5. DNA Precipitation: Add ethanol or isopropanol to precipitate DNA
  6. Purification: Wash with 70% ethanol and resuspend in buffer

Bacterial DNA Isolation Flowchart

Bacterial Culture ‚Üì Centrifuge (Collect Pellet) ‚Üì Lysozyme + SDS (Cell Lysis) ‚Üì Proteinase K (Protein Digestion) ‚Üì Phenol-Chloroform Extraction ‚Üì Ethanol Precipitation ‚Üì Purified Chromosomal DNA

1.2 Isolation of Plasmid DNA from Bacteria

Plasmid DNA is smaller and circular, making it easier to isolate separately from chromosomal DNA.

Key Methods for Plasmid DNA Isolation:
  • Alkaline Lysis Method: Most common method
    • Uses NaOH + SDS to denature DNA and proteins
    • Chromosomal DNA denatures and precipitates
    • Plasmid DNA renatures and stays in solution
  • Boiling Method: Quick but less pure
  • Commercial Kits: Use silica columns for purification
Feature Chromosomal DNA Plasmid DNA
Size Large (millions of bp) Small (thousands of bp)
Shape Linear Circular
Copy Number One per cell Multiple (10-100s)
Isolation Method Complete cell lysis Selective alkaline lysis

1.3 Isolation of DNA from Yeast

Yeast cells have a rigid cell wall made of glucan and chitin, requiring special treatment for DNA isolation.

Yeast DNA Isolation Protocol:
  1. Cell Wall Digestion: Use zymolyase or lyticase enzymes
  2. Spheroplast Formation: Cell wall removed, creating spheroplasts
  3. Lysis: Use detergents (SDS) or mechanical disruption
  4. Protein Removal: Phenol-chloroform extraction
  5. DNA Precipitation: Ethanol precipitation
  6. RNase Treatment: Remove RNA contamination

2. Bacterial Restriction and Modification Systems

Bacteria have evolved a defense mechanism called Restriction-Modification (R-M) system to protect themselves from foreign DNA (like bacteriophages).

Two Components of R-M System:
  1. Restriction Enzymes: Cut foreign DNA at specific sequences
  2. Modification Enzymes: Methylate (protect) host DNA at the same sequences

2.1 How R-M System Works:

Host Bacterial DNA ‚Üì (Methylated by Modification Enzyme) Protected DNA (Not cut) Foreign DNA (Phage DNA) ‚Üì (Not methylated) Recognized by Restriction Enzyme ‚Üì (Cut into fragments) Foreign DNA Destroyed
Key Points:
  • Modification enzymes add methyl groups to specific DNA sequences
  • Methylated DNA is protected from restriction enzymes
  • Foreign DNA lacks methylation and gets destroyed
  • This is a bacterial immune system against bacteriophages

3. Restriction Endonucleases and Their Types

Restriction endonucleases (restriction enzymes) are molecular scissors that cut DNA at specific recognition sequences. They are the foundation of recombinant DNA technology.

4.2 Sticky Ends vs Blunt Ends

Sticky Ends (Cohesive Ends):
  • Have single-stranded overhangs
  • Complementary ends can base-pair
  • Easier to ligate together
  • Example: EcoRI creates 5'-AATT-3' overhang
Blunt Ends:
  • No overhangs - cut straight through both strands
  • Less specific ligation
  • Require higher ligase concentration
  • Example: SmaI cuts both strands at same position

EcoRI Cutting Example:

Original DNA: 5'-G AATTC-3' 3'-CTTAA G-5' After EcoRI Cut: 5'-G AATTC-3' 3'-CTTAA G-5' ‚Üë ‚Üë Sticky ends (complementary) These ends can easily pair with other EcoRI-cut fragments!

4.3 Naming Convention

Restriction enzymes are named based on:

  • Genus: First letter (E = Escherichia)
  • Species: Next two letters (co = coli)
  • Strain: Next letter (R = strain RY13)
  • Number: Roman numeral (I = first enzyme discovered)
  • Example: EcoRI = Escherichia coli R strain, enzyme I

5. DNA Ligation Reaction Mechanism of DNA Ligases

DNA ligase is the enzyme that joins DNA fragments together by forming phosphodiester bonds. It's like molecular glue that seals nicks in DNA.

5.1 Types of DNA Ligases

Type Source Characteristics Uses
T4 DNA Ligase Bacteriophage T4
  • Most commonly used
  • Works on both sticky and blunt ends
  • Requires ATP
  • Active at 16¬∞C
 General cloning
E. coli DNA Ligase E. coli
  • Requires NAD+ (not ATP)
  • Works mainly on sticky ends
  • Less efficient for blunt ends
 Specific applications
Thermostable Ligases Thermophilic bacteria
  • Active at high temperatures
  • Used in ligation cycling
 Specialized techniques

5.2 Mechanism of DNA Ligation

T4 DNA Ligase Mechanism (3 Steps):
  1. Activation:
    • Ligase binds to ATP (or NAD+ for E. coli ligase)
    • Adenosine monophosphate (AMP) is transferred to lysine residue in ligase
    • Formation of ligase-AMP complex
  2. AMP Transfer:
    • AMP is transferred from ligase to 5' phosphate of DNA
    • Formation of DNA-AMP intermediate (pyrophosphate bond)
    • This activates the 5' phosphate group
  3. Phosphodiester Bond Formation:
    • The activated 5' phosphate attacks the 3' OH group
    • Phosphodiester bond is formed
    • AMP is released
    • Nick is sealed!

DNA Ligation Mechanism:

Step 1: Activation Ligase + ATP ‚Üí Ligase-AMP + PPi Step 2: AMP Transfer to DNA Ligase-AMP + DNA (5'-P, 3'-OH) ‚Üí Ligase + DNA-AMP (activated) Step 3: Bond Formation DNA-AMP + adjacent 3'-OH ‚Üí Sealed DNA + AMP Result: Phosphodiester bond formed! 5'-...P-O-P-O-P...-3' ‚Üë ‚Üë Sealed nick

5.3 Ligation Conditions

Optimal Conditions for T4 DNA Ligase:
  • Temperature: 16¬∞C (overnight) or 22-25¬∞C (1-2 hours)
  • Buffer: Tris-HCl (pH 7.5-8.0), Mg¬≤‚Å∫, DTT, ATP
  • DNA Ratio: Insert:Vector = 3:1 to 5:1 (for sticky ends)
  • DNA Ratio: Insert:Vector = 10:1 or higher (for blunt ends)
  • Time: 1-16 hours depending on DNA concentration

5.4 Ligation Products

After ligation, you can get several products:

  • Recombinant plasmid: Vector + insert (desired product)
  • Recircularized vector: Vector without insert (unwanted)
  • Insert dimers: Multiple inserts joined together (unwanted)
  • Vector concateners: Multiple vectors joined (unwanted)
Why Sticky Ends Ligate Better:
  • Complementary overhangs form hydrogen bonds
  • This holds fragments together temporarily
  • Ligase can then seal the nick more easily
  • Blunt ends have no such stabilizing interactions

6. Joining of DNA Fragments Using Linkers, Adaptors and Homopolymer Tails

When DNA fragments don't have compatible restriction sites, we need special methods to join them. Three main techniques are used:

6.1 Linkers

Linkers are short, double-stranded DNA oligonucleotides that contain one or more restriction enzyme recognition sites.

How Linkers Work:
  1. Synthesize linkers with desired restriction site (e.g., EcoRI site)
  2. Phosphorylate linkers at 5' end using polynucleotide kinase
  3. Ligate linkers to blunt-ended DNA fragments (using T4 DNA ligase)
  4. Cut with restriction enzyme (e.g., EcoRI) to create sticky ends
  5. Ligate to vector with compatible sticky ends

Linker Method:

Blunt-ended Insert DNA ‚Üì + Linker (EcoRI site) + ATP ‚Üì (T4 DNA ligase) DNA with Linker attached ‚Üì EcoRI digestion ‚Üì DNA with EcoRI sticky ends ‚Üì Ligate to EcoRI-cut vector ‚Üì Recombinant DNA
Advantages of Linkers:
  • ‚úì Can add any restriction site to blunt-ended DNA
  • ‚úì Relatively simple and inexpensive
  • ‚úì Multiple linkers can be added (concatenation)
Disadvantages:
  • ‚úó The restriction site becomes part of the insert
  • ‚úó If insert contains the same restriction site, it will be cut
  • ‚úó Requires phosphorylation step

6.2 Adaptors

Adaptors are short, double-stranded oligonucleotides that already have one sticky end ready for ligation. They avoid the need for restriction enzyme digestion after ligation.

How Adaptors Work:
  1. Synthesize adaptor with:
    • One end: Blunt or compatible with insert
    • Other end: Sticky end (e.g., EcoRI sticky end)
  2. Ligate adaptor to insert DNA (using T4 DNA ligase)
  3. Result: Insert now has sticky end
  4. Ligate directly to vector with compatible sticky end

Adaptor Method:

Blunt-ended Insert DNA ‚Üì + Adaptor (one blunt end, one EcoRI sticky end) ‚Üì (T4 DNA ligase - only ligates blunt ends) Insert with Adaptor ‚Üì Direct ligation to EcoRI-cut vector ‚Üì Recombinant DNA Note: Adaptor already has sticky end! No need for restriction enzyme cutting.
Feature Linkers Adaptors
Structure Blunt on both ends Blunt on one end, sticky on other
After Ligation Still blunt-ended Has sticky end
Restriction Enzyme Needed? Yes (after ligation) No
Advantage Flexible - add any site Faster - no digestion step
Problem with Adaptors:

Self-ligation: Adaptors can ligate to each other, forming dimers. To prevent this:

  • Don't phosphorylate the sticky end of adaptor
  • Only the blunt end gets ligated to insert
  • The sticky end remains unligated until it meets the vector

6.3 Homopolymer Tails

Homopolymer tailing (also called dG-dC tailing) uses complementary homopolymeric sequences to join DNA fragments.

How Homopolymer Tailing Works:
  1. Add poly(dG) to vector:
    • Cut vector to create 3' OH ends
    • Add multiple G nucleotides using terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT)
    • Creates vector with poly(dG) tails
  2. Add poly(dC) to insert:
    • Prepare insert DNA with 3' OH ends
    • Add multiple C nucleotides using TdT
    • Creates insert with poly(dC) tails
  3. Hybridization:
    • Mix vector and insert
    • Poly(dG) and poly(dC) anneal (G-C base pairing)
    • Forms hybrid molecule
  4. Gap Repair:
    • Gaps filled by DNA polymerase
    • Nicks sealed by DNA ligase
    • Recombinant DNA complete!

Homopolymer Tailing Method:

Vector DNA (with 3'-OH) ‚Üì (TdT + dGTP) Vector-poly(dG) tail 5'-...OH-(G)n-3' Insert DNA (with 3'-OH) ‚Üì (TdT + dCTP) Insert-poly(dC) tail 5'-...OH-(C)n-3' ‚Üì (Mix and anneal) Hybrid Formation: Vector-(G)n...(C)n-Insert ‚Üì (DNA polymerase + ligase) Complete Recombinant DNA
Advantages of Homopolymer Tailing:
  • ‚úì Works with any DNA (no need for restriction sites)
  • ‚úì Can't recut with restriction enzymes (no restriction site added)
  • ‚úì Creates strong hydrogen bonding (G-C pairs)
  • ‚úì Good for directional cloning
Disadvantages:
  • ‚úó Cannot easily remove the insert later
  • ‚úó Creates unpaired regions that need repair
  • ‚úó Less efficient than sticky-end ligation
